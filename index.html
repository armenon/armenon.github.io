Baby Location
Complete Medical Solutions

  • Client
    • District Health Board, Manukau County, Auckland, New Zealand
  • References
    • Apple store
    • Web link
  • Role
    • UI/UX Designer
    • Visual Designer
    • Mobile Application Developer
  • Time
    • 1.5 years (including pilot study)
  • Abstract
    • Baby Location is an iOS application that interfaces with an Internet of Things (IoT) based tracking platform to track the location of newborn infants in the hospitals coming under the jurisdiction of the District Health Board of Manukau County in Auckland, New Zealand.
    • My Role
      • I was involved in both designing and developing the interface for the application and the overall user experience including how users interacted with various technical artefacts in the system.
    • Why was this needed?
      • The District Health Board of Manukau County wanted to increase the security at the hospitals under their jurisdiction. They wanted to harness the power of the Internet of Things to see if they could install a future proof system that could ultimately be used to improve their overall security system. To this end, they called out to many companies to test their solutions as a proof of concept at one of the biggest hospitals under their care - Middlemore Hospital.
    • The Pilot Study
      • Complete Medical Solutions and WiSilica undertook a month long pilot study at Middlemore Hospital, Auckland in order to test and prove the effectiveness of their solution. The pilot study involved using a prototype application that I designed.
      • The prototype application evolved into one of the first versions of the tracking applications used by WiSilica.
    • The Issues
      • The primary users of this system were the nurses at the respective hospitals, the system was planned to be installed. However, due to their busy schedules the overhead introduced by the new system would have been an additional burden for them.The first version had flows that were too long winded and complicated for them to use. So the system had to be redesigned to be quick and simple to use.
      • The administrators of the system also needed an interface to install, configure and monitor the system, which required a more technical interface. Thus the interface had a dichotomy between having to be simple and having to be technical
      • One of the main complaints that the nurses had during the pilot study was that system was too technically challenging to use. The nurses were people who were just about comfortable with smartphones but not more. The flow had to be redesigned so that the system did most of the heavy lifting and was also ‘technically simple’ to use
    • Solution Goals
      • Simplify the application’s functionality to show primary goals in the first UI
      • Perform complex actions with simple steps
      • Use a simple design language
      • Use illustrations to indicate actions
      • Role based UIs
    • Core Features
      • Login
      • Prep Screen
      • Add a baby tag
      • Track a Baby
      • Alerts
      • Technical Screens
        • Add Devices
        • Diagnostics
        • Users
    • Evaluations and Feedback
      • Owing to the difficulties in logistics, proper evaluations and usability tests could not be conducted. The majority of the testing done was functional and done remotely, in India.
      • The nurses responded very positively to the changes in UX and the design language. They felt that the new application was easy to use and visually appealing.
      • The management at the District Health Board were also pleased with the functioning of the system and application and were considering utilising the system to track all the assets in the hospital

  • Abstract
    • Group based searching allows users of streaming services such as Netflix to able to decide upon what media to watch in a more simpler way.
    • Group Members

      • Fredilyn Villarin
      • Judith Sirera I Pulido
    • My Role
      • I collaborated to create the interaction and flow for the interface. I also created the final interactive prototype.

      • Not to create another app
      • Not to sacrifice the social aspect of discussing the pros and cons of what to watch
      • Reduce the 18 minutes that was spent on deciding what to watch
      • Make the system do the heavy lifting
      • Design for smart tvs

      • Members add filters and preferences
      • Suggested list
      • Member Voting
      • Results
    • Evaluations and Feedback
      • Primary evaluations and testing gave us the following results -
        • “Consistent”
        • “User Friendly”
        • “Practical”

EFFECT OF COLOR ON HUMAN PERCEPTION
Work done at KTH Royal Institute of Technology

  • Work done as a part of
    • Human Perception for Information Technology (DT2350) course at KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm
  • Poster
    • Research Poster
  • Role
    • - UI/UX Design (Mobile)
    • - Study design
    • - Research
    • - Application Development
  • Time
    • 1.5 months
  • Abstract
    • Colour is one of most important properties relating to vision. It is a preattentive feature that is processed at the retina itself. In the context of IT, colour is pervasive in almost everything. This project aims to identify the impacts of colour in the context of IT by using simple web-based and mobile application based memory games.
  • Group Members
    • Lucas Ahlgren
    • Melissa Marin
    • Erna Guðrún Sigurðardóttir
  • My Role
    • I helped create the study and also developed the mobile application which was used as a part of our study. Additionally, I also helped analyse the results and put them into context.
  • Why was this needed?
    • TWe wanted to find out how much colour affected our perception, in the context of IT, so that designers and developers could use it in a more judicious way when developing new interfaces.
  • Goals
    • - Create questions designed like memory tests
    • - Subtly involve the use of colour to test user perception
    • - Make it a game so that people will be encouraged to participate
  • Technical Features
    • Timed memory test game on both web and mobile platforms
  • Result
    • Ultimately we were able to conclude from our study that while colour does have an effect on memory and perception, our study could not prove it to be statistically significant. However, we did notice that other factors of UI elements such as position and size had an effect on memory and perception

Evaluating voice assistants for day-to-day activities
Work done at KTH Royal Institute of Technology

  • Work done as a part of
    • Evaluation Methods in Human Computer Interaction (DH2632) course at KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm
  • Presentation
    • Presentation
  • Group Members
    • Rafael Lucerna Araujo
    • Martin Tournier
    • Rhodie Khodrs
  • Role
    • Research
    • Usability Testing
  • Time
    • 1.5 months
  • Abstract
    • Voice assistants are becoming more prevalent and capable, providing designers with a new interface or platform to utilise. Smartphone voice based assistants are available to nearly everyone who owns a smartphone. This project aims to test the usability of the assistants for routine everyday tasks
    • My Role
      • I helped design and conduct the usability study and also to analyse the results
    • Why was this needed?
      • We wanted to test if voice assistants were ready to accomplish everyday tasks that require the use of of a visual interface.
      • We also wanted to gather insights regarding the use of Siri and Google for such tasks
    • The Pilot Study
      • We conducted a pilot study with participants to test the effectiveness of our usability study. We found that the participants themselves get flustered when the voice assistants fail to perform the required task and when repeating the task, participants tend phrase the request differently.
    • Study Goals
      • - Evaluate if voice assistants are ready to be replace screen based interfaces
      • - Check the usability of Siri and Google Assistant
      • - Use a common language (English) for the study
      • - Create simple usability tests that reflect everyday tasks
      • - Evaluate if Siri or Google have an edge over each other
    • Study Tasks
      • Simple tasks - One-off tasks that are independent of other tasks and boil down to the speech recognition capability of the voice assistant
      • Contextual tasks - Tasks that involve a previously executed task and thereby measure the assistant’s ability to understand context
    • Evaluations and Results
      • - From our study, we had to conclude that while voice based interfaces have come a long way in terms of capability and accessibility, they are still not fully ready to be used for everyday tasks.
      • - We were also able to conclude that while Siri has an overall slight edge over Google assistant in this study, it was not statistically significant.
      • - However, we did find a statistical significance in Google assistant’s ability to accurately understand speech over Siri.
      • - But Siri was able to infer context better, though there was no statistical significance for the conclusion.

USING AUGMENTED REALITY & SONIFICATION TO ENHANCE TABLETOP GAMES
Work done at KTH Royal Institute of Technology

  • Work done as a part of
    • Multimodal Interaction and Interfaces (DT2140)course at KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm
  • References
    • Presentation
  • Role
    • - Research
    • - Application Development
  • Time
    • 2 months
  • Abstract
    • Augmented Reality provides an additional layer for designers to manipulate in order to add interactivity to a product. With the rise of AR on smartphones, the technology is now more accessible than ever. This project explores how AR and sounds can be used to enhance and improve comprehension using a tabletop game called ‘Munchkin’ as a proof of concept.
    • Group Members
      • Akash Menon
      • Alexander Persson
      • Lisa Lund
    • My Role
      • I helped design the flows and also to develop the mobile application
    • Why was this needed?
      • Information is most useful when provided in the right context. As mentioned before, new technologies such as Augmented Reality provides an additional interface for designers to add a new layer of interactivity to a product. However, a lot of the applications currently using AR choose to make the AR interface the primary interface for interacting with the product, which robs users of the benefits they get when interacting with the objects in real-time space. We wanted to challenge this screen based thinking by exploring how AR can be used instead as a secondary support interface so as enhance comprehension.
      • ‘Munchkin’ is a tabletop card game that has a LOT of rules that need to be understood both before and while playing the game. We wanted the AR and sonification interface to essentially replace the rulebook and allow player to jump right and learn as they play.
    • Concept Goals
      • - NOT port the game into a digital version
      • - The AR and sound interface must serve to enhance the original gameplay (with the cards. However, it should NOT be necessary to play it.
      • - The system should provide contextual information as people play.
      • - The system should replace the rulebook and allow users to dive right in and learn as they play.
      • - Sound effects should serve to provide information to players who cannot view the AR interface
    • Core Features
      • - Card Details on AR interface
      • - Contextual Effects as the user plays the game
      • - Sound effects for actions
    • Evaluations and Feedback
      • Unfortunately, we did not have the time to conduct proper usability and evaluation tests as we only created a proof of concept.
      • However, informal testing and impressions showed that people understood how to play ‘Munchkin’ much better from using the AR interface than by reading the rule book.
      • Future work includes improving the interface and getting user feedback to improve the overall service.

WEARABLE JACKET WITH INTEGRATED FALL SENSOR AND MUSCLE STIMULATOR
Work done at Government Model Engineering College, Thrikkakara

  • Work done as a part of
    • Undergraduate Thesis at Government Model Engineering College, Thrikkakara
  • Group Members
    • Aswin Kariat
    • Albert Alani
    • Pooja K A
    • Jeswy Thankam Shaji
  • Role
    • Electronics & Circuit Design
    • Programming
    • Product Design
  • Time
    • 4-5 months
  • Abstract
    • This wearable jacket, intended for the elderly, for hosptial patients, etc is designed to detect if the user falls down and alerts people to the same. It also contains a muscular pain relief electo-stimulator
    • The jacket contains an integrated fall detection sensor (using an accelerometer) that is connected to a GSM module capable of sending a text message to emergency contacts. The jackt also contains a integrated electronic muscle stimulator, that when activated, passes a small electric current through the arm muscles that is capable of muscular pain relief.
    • My Role
      • I was invovled in all aspects of creation, from designing the actual product to creating the electronic circuits and programming the system.
    • Why was this needed?
      • Elderly citizens and hospital patients who fall down are sometimes unable to get the immediate assistance that they require. Lack of timely assistance can exacerbate the problem and even lead to the patient's demise. The jacket is designed to help caregivers and relatives of such people by providing them with timely notifications of fall events. Additionally, people who suffer from muscular cramps, tend to benefit from the pain relief provided by eletrical muscle stimulation. This jacket tries to incorporate a miniature version of the same.
      • The jacket is also intended for patients with muscular degenerative diseases such as Motor Neuron Disease (MND), which in addition to causing the patient to gradually lose control of their volutary motions, also causes loss muscle tone due to lack of use. Therefore, a caretaker has to regulary move the limbs of the patient to prevent this. The integrated muscular stimulator can prevent this loss of muscle tone, saving the caretaker from manually having to move the patient's limbs and the fall detection can be used to detect if the patient has fallen from their chair or bed, especially at night, since the patient gradually becomes unable to speak
    • Solution Goals
      • - Simple design
      • - Wearable and portable
      • - Try to make the system modular, so that different sensors can be integrated
    • Core Features
      • Fall detection (using an accelerometer) with alerting
      • GSM Module based SMS alerts to pre-stored contacts
      • Integrated electronic muscle stimulation
      • Wearble computer (Arduino Lilypad)
    • Evaluations and Feedback
      • Owing to the difficulties in testing medical products, especially on the intended target audience, we could perform proper usabilty tests and evaluations. However, we self-tested the product and were able to verify that the all modules worked under a variety of conditions.
      • We successfully presented this product for our thesis to panel of judges and evaluators
